The Eleventh Annual NYCAASC: REALITIES

2016 was an extremely divisive year - it revealed how little we truly know of our neighbors, even in New York City.

Our city is segregated by our various identities, like race and class. Although we share a single city border, we hardly know our neighbors beyond appearances and preconceived notions.

This year’s conference will explore the theme of REALITIES -- By delving into the experiences, histories, and communities of our families and neighbors, NYCAASC 2017 aims to identify and question the power structures that shape our realities.

What determines our realities?

Which realities are considered “authentic”?

And most importantly, whose realities become history?

Through the power of communal storytelling, REALITIES invites attendees to challenge our perceived realities of one another and to mobilize our communities beyond borders.

NYCAASC 2017: REALITIES Programming

Keynote Speaker

Allan Isaac

Allan Punzalan Isaac is Associate Professor of American Studies and English and Chair of American Studies at Rutgers University. He is the author of American Tropics: Articulating Filipino America (2006), which received the Association for Asian American Studies Cultural Studies Book Award. He has taught at LaSalle University in Manila as a Senior Fulbright Scholar. His current work focuses on labor-time and Filipino migrant labor.

Performers

Tim Atlas

Tim Atlas is an Indie singer-songwriter from the Silicon Valley, currently living in Los Angeles. Tim has found musical inspiration in the airy falsettos of Damien Rice and the endearingly wistful pop melodies of Feist. In 2016, he released “Wander,” a single that premiered at #19 on the Spotify Global Viral Charts with over 1 million streams since its release in July 2016. While on a North American tour with Daniela Andrade, he released “Compromised”, a song that now sits at 2.3 million plays on Spotify. When Atlas isn’t performing as a solo artist, he works as a producer and writer in his home studio. He is currently gearing up to record his next EP, incorporating a more rhythmic, soulful approach, without straying from the folk elements his fans have grown to love.

Kim Chinh

Kim Chinh began her acting career at the Alley Theater in Houston, Texas. She has appeared in several shorts including The Face written and directed by Elizabeth Browning, L to Canarsie, and See Something Say Something both written and directed by Alice Cox. On stage, she played Maggie in The Chinese Restaurant Syndrome directed by April Feld Sandor at the Walnut Street Theatre in Philadelphia. She played Lia in Big Flower Eater written and directed by Victoria Linchong. Cesi Davidson, playwright, has featured Kim in several of her story-telling series and staged readings. Kim participated in New York’s Summerstages as a cast member of SPEAK featuring the music and spoken word of Marla Mase and the Tomas Doncker band. Kim’s solo show, Reclaiming Vietnam, has been performed at the New York and Edinburgh Fringe Festivals, and will be seen on the west coast this September at the San Francisco Fringe. Her play has been performed for students at Fordham University and St. Joseph's College with an upcoming performance at Kingsborough Community College in May. She is thrilled and honored to have the opportunity to present her work to the NYCAASC.

Brett Kodama

Brett Ryoji Kodama is a Japanese American filmmaker based in New York City. He graduated from The School of Visual Arts in 2015, earning a BFA in Film and Video, specializing in Film Editing. During his time in school, he learned about all aspects of the filmmaking process, including writing, directing, cinematography, editing, and more. He has worked on a number of projects including short films, music videos, live events, and even a TV pilot.

Currently, he is working as a freelance cinematographer and editor in New York City.



Featured Events

One-Two-One-Seven

A first-hand account of living through the Japanese-American Incarceration Camps.

Sharon Shizuko Okazaki Kodama was only three-years old when she and her family, Family No. 1217, were incarcerated at the Manzanar Relocation Center in 1942. She remained there until the end of the war in August 1945.

One-Two-One-Seven is not only a personal story to me, it is also one of importance for the country. The modern day incarceration of 120,000 civilians based on their ethnicity is something that should be common knowledge, but it is not; instead, it is ignored, brushed aside, or belittled. The internment camps are something that, especially given recent events concerning talks of creating a Muslim Registry, should be taught in schools. It is hard to grow as a country by ignoring past mistakes only to make them again.

Brett Kodama, director

HSL Gallery

"The High School Liaison program brings together high school youth from across the city to participate in weekly workshops on political education & arts-based learning. We have covered topics like Asian American history, the concept of “home” and migration, structural racism, and feminism/ gender feels. In our time together, we have produced mini-zines, a patchwork quilt, group poems, and performed stories, contributing to the larger culture of Asian America. We invite you to interact with and add-on to our creations, whether it’s stitching a patch to our gender quilt, striking up a conversation with someone else present in the gallery, or sounding off on the prompts posted in our space.

Within the gallery is also a video series we’ve produced. Our videos focus on the unique experiences that form each HSLers understanding of Asian American identity and how this intersects with their everyday lives. Living in different social, economic, and political environments, each member tells their own story about issues that relate back to the Asian American community. We invite you to tune in and add your own thoughts or experiences to the Asian American lives presented on screen."

– 2017 High School Liaison directors, Ying Yu Situ & Alina Shen

Frequently Asked Questions

  • What is NYCAASC?

    The New York City Asian American Student Conference (NYCAASC) is NYC’s premier conference for exposing bright, young minds to issues that affect the Asian/Pacific Islander American community.

  • Where are we?

    We wouldn’t be NYCAASC without the NYC. The conference takes place at the NYU Kimmel Center for Student Life, which is located near the following subway stations: West 4 St - Washington Square (accessible via the A, B, C, D, F, & M), Astor Place (6), and 8 St - NYU (R and W).

  • When is NYCAASC 2017 happening?

    Mark your calendars for April 29, 2017! The conference is all day long, from 8:30 AM to 6:00 PM.

  • I’m not an Asian/Pacific Islander American. Can I still attend the conference?

    NYCAASC is run by APIA students, but is open to anyone interested in learning about Asian America, regardless of student status, race, or ethnicity!

  • How do I register?

    Click here to RSVP for the conference on Eventbrite. Just select the number of tickets you want, fill out your information, and you’re good to go!

  • How much is the conference?

    It’s FREE!

  • Really? I don’t have to pay anything?

    Really really.

  • Will there be food?

    YES! Broke college students, have no fear. We provide FREE breakfast, FREE lunch, and FREE snacks and refreshments!

  • Does NYCAASC host events other than the main conference in April?

    We have hosted a variety of satellite events alongside fellow APIA student clubs in the past, including open mics with a variety of headliners and rock climbing fundraisers!

    The best way to stay informed on outside events is to like on Facebook!

  • How can I support NYCAASC?

    The easiest way to support us is to just attend our conference! If you, for some reason, can’t make it to NYCAASC, you can always like and share our event on Facebook, Twitter, and Instagram to help us spread the word about our conference; our conference’s longevity and success are dependent on YOU!

    If you want to help empower and inspire students in NYC, please support our 2017 conference at our crowdfunding campaign. More information about NYCAASC funding can be found on our budget page.
